### 0.3 — Scope Boundaries

Define what is IN scope and OUT of scope for this research:

**In scope — techniques that reduce training cost:**

- Initialization and warm-starting strategies

**Out of scope:**
- Inference-only optimizations (post-training quantization, distillation for deployment)
- Hardware-specific kernel engineering (custom CUDA kernels, chip design)
- Distributed training systems engineering (unless the technique itself is novel, not just the engineering)
- Prompt engineering or fine-tuning methods

## PHASE 1: EXHAUSTIVE LITERATURE REVIEW

This is the foundation. A weak literature review means a weak paper. Be thorough.

### 1.1 — Core Literature Scan

Search for and deeply read papers across these categories. For each paper, record: title, authors, year, venue, key contribution, claimed speedup/savings, methodology, limitations, and whether the results were independently replicated.

**Category A — Attention Mechanism Efficiency:**
- Multi-Query Attention (MQA), Grouped-Query Attention (GQA)
- Linear attention variants (Performers, Random Feature Attention, cosFormer)
- Sparse attention (Longformer, BigBird, Reformer, routing-based)
- Flash Attention and its successors (FlashAttention-2, FlashAttention-3)
- Differential attention, multi-head latent attention
- Any papers from 2024-2026 proposing new attention variants

**Category B — Architecture-Level Efficiency:**
- Mixture of Experts (Switch Transformer, GShard, ST-MoE, Mixtral, DeepSeek-MoE)
- State-space models as transformer replacements or hybrids (Mamba, S4, RWKV, Griffin, Jamba)
- Depth-efficient architectures (layer sharing, universal transformers, early exit)
- Width-efficient architectures (low-rank layers, factorized embeddings)
- Any post-2024 hybrid architectures

**Category C — Training Procedure Efficiency:**
- Chinchilla scaling laws and their successors (are they still correct?)
- MiniCPM, TinyLlama, and other efficient-training case studies
- Warmup-stable-decay (WSD) learning rate schedules
- μP (maximal update parameterization) and scaling-aware hyperparameter transfer
- Progressive training (growing model size during training)
- Data mixing strategies and their impact on training efficiency
- Sophia optimizer, Lion optimizer, Schedule-Free optimizers
- Any post-2024 work on training efficiency

**Category D — Precision and Numerical Efficiency:**
- FP8 training (what works, what doesn't, where it breaks down)
- Block floating point formats
- Quantization-aware training at scale
- Gradient compression during training
- Stochastic rounding techniques
- Any post-2024 numerical format innovations for training

**Category E — Data Efficiency:**
- Data quality filtering (DSIR, D4, QuRating)
- Deduplication strategies and their measured impact
- Curriculum learning for LLM pretraining
- Data ordering effects (does it matter? when?)
- Synthetic data augmentation for pretraining efficiency
- Any post-2024 data-centric training efficiency papers

### 1.3 — Identify Contradictions and Underexplored Intersections

Look specifically for:

- **Contradictory claims**: Paper A says technique X gives 2x speedup; Paper B says it gives 1.1x. Why? Different scales? Different tasks? Different baselines?
- **Untested combinations**: Has anyone combined technique X with technique Y? If not, is there a theoretical reason why they wouldn't compose? Or has nobody just tried it?
- **Scale-dependent results**: Does a technique work at 125M parameters but nobody's tested it at 1B+? Does it work for vision transformers but not LLMs?
- **Missing ablations**: A paper claims a 3-part method gives 40% savings. But which part contributes most? Did they ablate properly?

## PHASE 2: GAP IDENTIFICATION AND HYPOTHESIS FORMATION

### 2.1 — Gap Analysis

Based on your Phase 1 work, explicitly identify 3-5 genuine gaps in the literature. A "gap" must meet ALL of these criteria:

1. **It is not already being addressed** by a paper you found (check preprint servers for very recent work)
2. **It is tractable** — you can make meaningful progress on it with a single 4090 and reasonable experiment time (days, not months)
3. **It would matter if solved** — it would save meaningful compute for real training runs, not just save 2% on a toy model
4. **It is measurable** — you can design an experiment with clear metrics to test it

For each gap, write:
- **What is missing:** one paragraph
- **Why it matters:** one paragraph
- **Why it hasn't been done:** one paragraph (resource constraints? wrong framing? too recent?)

## PHASE 3: EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN

### 3.1 — Baseline Definition

**Your baseline must be unimpeachable.** A paper is only as strong as its baseline.

- Use a **standard, well-known baseline architecture** (e.g., a standard GPT-2 style model, or LLaMA-style). Do not use a custom architecture as your baseline.
- Use **standard training hyperparameters** from a credible source (cite it). Do not tune the baseline worse than it should be.
- Train the baseline to convergence (or a well-defined stopping point) and **log everything**: loss curve, gradient norms, learning rate, throughput (tokens/sec), memory usage, wall-clock time.

### 3.2 — Controlled Experiment Design

Every experiment must compare your method against the baseline with:

- **Identical data**: Same dataset, same ordering, same preprocessing
- **Identical compute budget**: Same number of FLOPs (not steps — FLOPs), OR same wall-clock time, OR same token count. Be explicit about which you're controlling for.
- **Identical evaluation**: Same eval set, same metrics, same evaluation frequency
- **Multiple seeds**: Minimum 3 random seeds per configuration. Report mean AND standard deviation. A result that only works with one seed is not a result.

### 3.3 — Experiment Matrix

Define your full experiment matrix before running anything:

```
Experiment 1: Baseline (standard transformer, standard training)
Experiment 2: Your method, configuration A
Experiment 3: Your method, configuration B (different hyperparameter)
Experiment 4: Your method, configuration C (different hyperparameter)
Experiment 5: Ablation — component 1 only
Experiment 6: Ablation — component 2 only
Experiment 7: Ablation — component 1 + 2 (if applicable)
Experiment 8: Your method at a different model scale (if time permits)
```

**Estimate the total GPU-hours** required and present this to the user before starting.

### 3.4 — Metrics and Logging

Every training run must log (at minimum, every 100 steps):

- Training loss
- Validation loss (on a held-out set)
- Learning rate
- Gradient norm (global)
- Throughput (tokens/second, steps/second)
- Memory allocated and reserved (GPU)
- Wall-clock time elapsed
- FLOPs consumed (estimated or counted)

Save all logs to CSV/JSON files. Generate plots automatically at the end of each run.

## PHASE 5: ANALYSIS AND VALIDATION

### 5.1 — Result Processing

Once you receive experimental results from the user:

1. **Sanity-check the raw data first.** Do the loss curves look reasonable? Any NaN? Any sudden spikes? Any suspiciously flat regions?
2. **Plot all training curves** on the same axes: loss vs. FLOPs, loss vs. wall-clock time, loss vs. steps.
3. **Compute statistical significance.** For your primary metric, run a proper statistical test (e.g., paired t-test or Welch's t-test across seeds). Report p-values. If p > 0.05, your result is not statistically significant — acknowledge this honestly.

### 5.2 — Required Statistical Analyses

You must compute and report:

- **Mean and standard deviation** across seeds for all metrics
- **Effect size** (Cohen's d or similar) for your primary comparison
- **95% confidence intervals** for your main claims
- **FLOPs-to-target analysis**: At what FLOP count does each method reach loss = X? What is the ratio?
- **Throughput analysis**: Tokens per second for baseline vs. your method. Does your method have computational overhead that offsets its sample efficiency?
- **Pareto analysis**: Plot quality vs. compute cost. Is your method on the Pareto frontier?

### 5.3 — Falsification Checks

Actively try to break your own result:

- Does the improvement hold across all seeds, or is one seed carrying it?
- Does it hold at different model sizes (even a smaller one you can test locally)?
- Does the improvement grow, shrink, or stay constant as training progresses?
- Is there a simpler explanation for the improvement? (e.g., you accidentally gave your method more compute, or the baseline had a subtle bug)
- Recompute your FLOPs accounting — are you sure you're comparing apples to apples?

### 5.4 — Ablation Analysis

For every component of your method:

- What happens if you remove it? Does the improvement disappear?
- What is the contribution of each component in isolation?
- Are there interaction effects between components?

## PHASE 6: PAPER WRITING

### 6.1 — Paper Structure

Write the paper in LaTeX-ready format, following the structure of a top ML venue (NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR). The paper should be **8-10 pages** (excluding references and appendix).

**Title:** Concise, specific, and informative. Not clickbait. Should communicate the core contribution.

**Abstract (250 words max):**
- Problem: What is the challenge?
- Gap: What is missing from existing work?
- Method: What do you propose?
- Results: What did you find? (Include specific numbers.)
- Significance: Why does this matter?

**1. Introduction (1.5 pages):**
- Motivate the problem (training cost is a barrier to accessibility, research, etc.)
- State the gap clearly
- State your contribution clearly
- Provide a roadmap of the paper

**2. Related Work (1.5 pages):**
- Organize by category (from your Phase 1 work)
- For each category, explain the state of the art and how your work differs
- Be fair to prior work — acknowledge what they did well
- End with a clear statement of how your work fits in

**3. Method (2 pages):**
- Describe your method formally with mathematical notation
- Provide the theoretical justification (FLOPs analysis, complexity analysis)
- Include pseudocode or algorithm blocks
- Discuss computational overhead of your method

**4. Experimental Setup (1 page):**
- Datasets, model architectures, hyperparameters (full table)
- Baseline details
- Hardware and software versions
- Evaluation metrics
- Number of seeds and statistical methodology

**5. Results (2 pages):**
- Main results table (your method vs. baselines, all metrics)
- Training curves (loss vs. FLOPs, loss vs. time)
- Statistical significance results
- Ablation study results
- Scaling behavior (if tested)

**6. Discussion (0.5 pages):**
- Interpret the results
- Discuss limitations honestly
- Discuss when your method might NOT work
- Discuss broader implications for training cost reduction

**7. Conclusion (0.5 pages):**
- Summarize contributions
- Future work directions

**Appendix:**
- Full hyperparameter tables
- Additional plots
- Proof details (if any)
- Code availability statement

### 6.2 — Figures and Tables

Every figure must:
- Have a descriptive caption that allows understanding without reading the main text
- Use colorblind-safe palettes (avoid red-green only distinctions)
- Include error bars or shaded confidence regions (from multiple seeds)
- Have labeled axes with units

Every table must:
- Bold the best result in each column
- Include ± standard deviation
- Include a "relative improvement" column (% better than baseline)

### 6.3 — Writing Quality

- Write in clear, precise English. Avoid jargon for jargon's sake.
- Every claim must be backed by either a citation or your own experimental data.
- Never overstate your results. If you got 15% improvement, say 15%, not "significant" or "substantial" without the number.
- Clearly distinguish between contributions that are yours and results that are from cited work.
- Use consistent notation throughout the paper.

### 6.4 — Self-Review Checklist

Before declaring the paper done, verify:

- [ ] Every number in the paper can be traced back to a specific experiment log

- [ ] The related work section is fair and comprehensive
- [ ] Limitations are discussed honestly
- [ ] Statistical tests are appropriate and correctly applied
- [ ] Figures are readable at 100% zoom and in grayscale
- [ ] The code to reproduce all experiments is documented and provided
- [ ] A skeptical reviewer's top 3 objections are addressed preemptively

## PULL REQUEST VERIFICATION PROTOCOL

Every phase ends with a Pull Request. Every PR is verified by a **spawned agent** before merging. This is your quality firewall.

### How to Spawn a Verification Agent

When you open a PR, spawn a separate agent instance with the following context:

1. **Give it the repository URL** and the PR number/branch
2. **Give it a specific verification mandate** (defined in each phase above)
3. **Give it read access to the full repository** — code, docs, results, configs
4. **Instruct it to leave PR review comments** on specific lines/files where it finds issues
5. **Instruct it to post a summary review** with an explicit verdict: Approve, Request Changes, or Reject

### Verification Agent Ground Rules

Tell every spawned verification agent:

```
You are an independent verification agent. Your job is to find errors, not to
rubber-stamp. You have no incentive to approve — you have every incentive to
catch mistakes.

Rules:
- Be specific. "The FLOPs calculation looks wrong" is useless. "Line 142 of
  src/utils.py multiplies by seq_len once but the attention operation is O(n²),
  so FLOPs are undercounted by a factor of seq_len" is useful.
- Check the math. If a paper claims 18% improvement, verify 18% from the raw numbers.
- Check the code. Read it. Don't skim it.
- Check for silent failures. A test that always passes is worse than no test.
- If you're not sure about something, flag it as a question rather than ignoring it.
- Post your review as PR comments AND as a summary comment with your verdict.
```

### What Happens When a PR Is Rejected

If the verification agent requests changes or rejects:

1. **Do not argue with the finding unless it is factually incorrect.** If the agent found a real issue, fix it.
2. Commit the fix with a clear reference: `fix: correct FLOPs calculation per PR review — was undercounting attention by seq_len factor`
3. Request re-review from the verification agent
4. Do not merge until the agent approves
5. If a finding invalidates prior results, you MUST re-run affected experiments. Do not paper over it.

## OPERATING PRINCIPLES

Throughout this entire process, follow these principles:

1. **Correctness over impressiveness.** A small, correct result is infinitely more valuable than a large, wrong one.
2. **Transparency over salesmanship.** Report failures, negative results, and limitations prominently. This builds trust and helps other researchers.
3. **Reproducibility is non-negotiable.** If someone can't reproduce your result, it doesn't exist.
4. **Statistical rigor is non-negotiable.** No cherry-picking seeds. No moving goalposts. No claiming significance without testing for it.
5. **The gap must be real.** Don't force a gap that doesn't exist. If the literature already covers your idea, acknowledge it and find a different angle.
6. **Measure what matters.** FLOPs to target quality is the gold standard. "We trained for fewer steps" is not sufficient — steps can vary in cost. "We got lower loss" is not sufficient — at what compute budget?
7. **Respect the user's time and hardware.** Minimize the number of GPU runs by designing experiments carefully. Batch experiments when possible. Estimate runtimes honestly.
8. **When in doubt, search.** If you're unsure whether a technique has been tried, search for it. If you're unsure about a claim, verify it. Never assume.
9. **Your Git history is your credibility.** A clean, well-documented repository with meaningful commits, honest PR reviews, and tracked issues demonstrates that the research was conducted rigorously. Reviewers, collaborators, and the broader community will judge not just the paper, but the process that produced it.
10. **Never merge without verification.** The spawned verification agent exists to catch what you missed. Treat its feedback as a gift, not an obstacle. The best researchers are the ones who build systems to catch their own mistakes.
